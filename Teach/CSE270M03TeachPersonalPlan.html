CSE 270: W03 Research: Software Methodology

The reading gave you a background in software life-cycle-based testing. Today, you and your partner(s) will select a software development methodology and research how it works with testing. At the end of the week, you will describe how to conduct testing activities within that methodology.

Select a Software Development Methodology

With your partner(s), select a software development methodology you would like to study this week. There are several options. Your job is to choose one. You can pick from the following list or select one of your own.

  • Code and Fix - Concept Dev, Code and Fix, Release
  • Waterfall – Requirements, Design, Implementation, Testing,
  • Spiral – Objectives, Planning, Risk Analysis, Prototyping, Requirement Validation, Design V&V, Simulations/Models/Benchmarks, Detailed Design, Unit test, Integration Test, Acceptance Test, Spirals 1,2,3,4
  • Incremental – Req, Analysis, Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, Maintenance
  • Agile- Brainstorm, Design, Develop, QA, Deployment
    • XP – Release Plan, Iteration Plan, Acceptance Test, Unit Test, Pair Programming
    • Scrum – Sprint Planning (Release Planning), Sprint (Iteration), Review (Test), Retrospective (Lesson learned, Objective)
  • Continuous Integrations and Development – Agile, Integration, Delivery, Deployment,
  • DevOps – Plan, Code, Build, Test, Release, Deploy, Operate, Monitor

The software has a lifecycle, much like a butterfly or moth. Each phase of the development lifecycle can use different methodologies. Each Methodology introduced more detail to each lifecycle phase.

  • Concept of Operation
    • Concept
      • The software concept is in Code & Fix, Spiral, and V-Model methodologies. The idea is one of the first stages of the customer's requirement, elicitating what they want and how they think it should work.
    • Software Objectives
      • Software Objectives is the next stage is figuring out the customer's general goals. Even though only spelled out in Spiral, it is required for all methodologies.
    • Risk
      • Risk is a common practice throughout all methodologies, but specifically to Spiral. All mature software projects are constantly identifying risks to mitigate issues and problems.
  • Requirements
    • System Requirements
      • System Requirements are a high-level list of requirements that usually identify hardware, operating system, communication, and architecture specifications. Specifically mentioned in the Waterfall, System Requirements are needed in all aspects of the Software Development Lifecycle.
    • Software Requirements
      • Software Requirements are specifications specific to the developed software. These requirements interact with the system requirements and identify all the functionality of the developed software. Waterfall and Spiral specifically call these out.
    • Architecture
      • The Architecture phase is the part of the System Requirements and is used to define better what resources the software will run on. Found primarily on V-Model explanations.
    • Requirements Validation
      • Requirements Validation lays out the preliminary research for testing the System and Software Requirements. It is necessary to try each requirement, known as verification. And that the requirements reflect what the customer wants, known as validation. Typically, this is done through a Software Requirements Review (SRR) meeting and is a significant milestone in the Software Lifecycle. This phase in the Spiral Methodology is precisely defined.
  • Design
    • Analysis
      • The analysis usually starts during the requirements phase and is finished during the development phase. It takes the requirements and begins the transformation process, breaking them down when programmers can begin implementing them in code. This process also addresses product, customer, data, and process workflows.
    • Program Design
      • The Program Design is a process of taking the requirements and coming up with solutions to implement them in code. Program Design, also called Product Design or Detailed Design, is introduced in Waterfall and further defined and explained in Spiral and V-Model methodologies.
    • Prototyping
      • Prototyping is a technique of creating representatives of the software functionality to show to the customer. Spiral Methodology heavily relies on the method used in Demonstrations during Agile. Prototypes do not typically work, and care should be taken to prevent customers from thinking it is the current working product.
    • Product Validation and Verification
      • As part of the design phase's last step, culminating in a Software Design Review meeting (Waterfall & Spiral), the team makes sure that the customer wants and the design fulfills requirements.
  • Implementation
    • Coding/Implementation
      • Coding and Implementation are where the tires meet the road. It tests if the Requirements and Design are detailed enough to translate the customer's needs down to executable binary code. Found in all methodologies, it is the most critical lifecycle phase.
    • Build
      • The Build phase is more complicated than just compiling the code. It involves making sure that it works with other parts of the System, Architecture, and Design. It correctly links all the pieces together into a simplified process. With the recent development of Continuous Integration and Delivery, this phase has come into its own.
  • Testing
    • Unit Testing
      • Unit Tests are typically written (before or) during (or after) the coding and implementation phase. Its primary focus is on Black Box testing each small coded component to verify that they work correctly.
    • Simulations/Simulators
      • Much like prototyping, simulator and simulations provide a way to verify that the system, architecture, and software work correctly. Specifically spelled out in Spiral, simulators help when the hardware is unavailable. Simulations model the system to provide performance benchmarks and are a valid testing method known as Testing by Analysis.
    • Integration Testing
      • As part of verifying the build phase, Integration Testing makes sure that interfaces between minor and major components are working together. That software properly talks to the architecture, and the system runs as designed.
    • System Testing
      • Found in Waterfall and V-Model methodologies, System Testing is a set of tests to ensure that the identified workflows are working as expected.
    • Acceptance Testing
      • Spiral and XP methodologies focus on Acceptance testing to verify and validate the originals customer's needs. Typically, a meeting, SAT (Software/System Acceptance Test), is done with the customer just before deployment. Walkthrough and demonstration tests address each of the customers' needs and concerns.
  • Operation and Maintenance
    • Deployment and Version Release
      • After acceptance with the customer, the customer received the software. The software package includes hardware (if needed), software packages for support, and scripts and programs to configure and set up the software. This phase addresses concerns of releases and DevOps. The XP methodology specifically requires a Release plan, and Agile addresses Deployment procedures.
    • Operation and Monitoring
      • The customer and user are using this stage of the software. Bugs are being found, and information and data are being sent back for analysis and fixes.

Find Sources

Once you and your partner(s) have selected a software development methodology, the next step is to find sources that describe it. There is a single paper written by the methodology author in some cases. You will certainly want to get that source if this is the case. In other cases, some works explain specific parts of the methodology. These can be very valuable. In other cases, some papers criticize or correct the process. Certainly, look for articles such as these.
You and your partner should have three to five sources when you are finished. Three might be sufficient if each of your citations is very detailed. You may need up to five if one or more of your sources do not cover the software methodology completely. You know you have enough when each fact is present in at least two sources.
Sources should be "high quality." This means that sources have known authorship (not Wikipedia) and the authors are experts. For example, if a source was published by a company (such as IBM), its reputation is behind the source so that it can be trusted. If the source was published in a journal, then the journal verifies that the author is a known expert. Finally, if the author is an expert in the field (this may require some investigation to verify this), the source can be trusted.

Make Notes

Carefully read each of your sources. Make special note of the following:

  • What are the checkpoints/milestones/phases of a project? (does the checkpoint have a name, how long is it, how do you know you reached it?)
  • What meetings need to take place? (who is required to attend, what will be the agenda, what you expect to be accomplished, how often or at what event will the meeting be called?)
  • What roles do people play? (what is the role’s name, what are the qualifications, what responsibilities are associated with the roles, who will be assigned to each role?)
  • What artifacts (documents, deliverables, products) are produced during the project? (who will author the document, who will read the document, what purpose does the document serve, is there a deadline?)
  • How is information communicated (such as status information, assignments, reports, etc.)?
  • How is testing performed?

As you answer each of these questions, write them down in a notes document. A note document is just a document where you jot down quotes, statistics, references, and facts that may come in handy for future use. This document will be submitted for a grade.
We will use this document for the end-of-week report. Pay special attention to the responsibilities of the tester in your methodology. Namely, their roles, the meetings they attend, documents they read and write, etc.
Only one document will be submitted for each partnership. This is a notes document, so it does not need to be professional. Feel free to dump information in a document and submit it. You will only be graded on the content, not on the presentation. Please submit a single PDF.
